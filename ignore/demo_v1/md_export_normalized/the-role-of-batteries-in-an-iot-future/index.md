# Overview

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to the multitude of “things” connected to the Internet, collecting and sharing information or allowing users to perform or initiate functions without being physically present. An automated smart home is an example of an IoT application. Analysts predict that there could be as many as **41.6 billion** connected IoT devices by 2025 across various industries and applications. Batteries will play a crucial role in the functionality of these devices.

# Detailed Explanation

The IoT encompasses devices that traditionally would not be connected to the Internet, such as smart light bulbs and security cameras. In healthcare, IoT devices like heart rate monitors enable remote patient monitoring, reducing the need for hospital visits. In agriculture, sensors can monitor environmental conditions to optimize crop growth. Manufacturing benefits from IoT through predictive maintenance of equipment.

One of the key challenges in advancing IoT is ensuring a reliable power source. While devices connected to a power supply face fewer issues, those in remote locations or mobile devices rely heavily on batteries. The longevity of these batteries is critical, with expected lifespans ranging from 3 to 10 years.

The ideal battery for IoT devices must consider the operating environment. Extreme temperatures can affect battery performance, making it essential for batteries to function efficiently in their intended conditions. Safety is also paramount, especially for wearable devices.

Developers are exploring ways to create batteries that last longer and are safer. Innovations include energy harvesting technologies that allow devices to recharge using solar, wind, or ambient sound energy. The goal is to develop a “forever” battery that eliminates the need for recharging or replacement.
