# Detailed Explanation

Most commercial batteries contain heavy metals, which can pose health and environmental risks if not disposed of properly. Lithium-ion batteries, for example, are generally safe while sealed, but the metals inside can become toxic when exposed. The accumulation of metals from discarded batteries can be dangerous, and their flammability poses additional risks.

In Europe, lithium-ion batteries are prohibited from landfills due to their toxicity and explosion risks. In the US, there have been incidents of recycling plants catching fire due to improperly disposed batteries. Lead-acid batteries, while recyclable, also pose risks when lead leaks into the environment, causing developmental issues, especially in children. Cadmium, found in some batteries, is even more toxic.

While 90% of lead-acid batteries are recycled, only 50% of lithium-ion batteries are, leading to significant waste of reusable materials. The recycling process for lithium-ion batteries is not profitable, as the costs often outweigh the value of the materials retrieved. Companies are working to improve this process as consumer products reach end-of-life.

Research is ongoing to develop non-toxic battery alternatives. For instance, a team at Imperial College London has created a prototype battery that uses plastic and salt water, allowing for quick charging and discharging. This battery is non-toxic and easily recyclable, although it currently has low energy density. Other research focuses on creating batteries that do not use nickel and cobalt, replacing them with safer materials.
