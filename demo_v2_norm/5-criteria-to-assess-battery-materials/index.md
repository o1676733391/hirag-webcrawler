# Overview

With researchers testing different materials for a fresh take on the battery, how should we assess whether a certain material is a viable option? Integrating materials like silicon, graphene, and even sodium into battery chemistries is currently being studied. Here are 5 factors to consider when assessing whether a certain battery material is feasible.

# Key Points

| Topic | Summary |
| Material cost | Material costs greatly affect the feasibility of using a certain material within batteries. In 2010, a lithium-ion battery pack cost more than USD1000/kwh. This dropped significantly to USD156/kwh by 2019, and is projected to drop below USD100/kwh in the coming years. The reducing cost of the battery has allowed more and more people to buy into batteries as a permanent energy solution for large applications such as electric vehicles or grid storage. However, certain materials commonly used within lithium-ion batteries contribute to the high cost of the battery. |
| Abundance and sustainability | The abundance and supply of a material not only affects its price but also long-term sustainability. Some analysts predict that the supply of the rare metals used in current batteries could deteriorate in just a few years. Industry insiders project that there would be a shortage of cobalt by 2022. Sodium is a strong rival to lithium in terms of long-term availability. The sodium-ion battery is potentially cheaper and easier to produce than its lithium-ion counterpart. |
| Material deterioration | Just because a material is abundant, it doesn’t mean it is necessarily suitable to use in a battery. Silicon tends to deteriorate quickly when used in a battery. Although its capacity is theoretically higher than graphite, the silicon anode tends to swell almost 300% when receiving ions, causing the anode to crack and deteriorate. Different materials react differently in different combinations. |
| Safety | The safety of the battery is a key concern. The dendrites formed inside the battery could potentially pierce the separator and cause the battery to short-circuit. Lithium-ion batteries have also been known to catch fire or combust when damaged. Semi-aqueous or solid-state electrolytes are viable replacements to create a safer battery. |
| Environmental impact | In large quantities, certain materials are toxic to the environment. Cobalt and nickel are currently being used in lithium-ion batteries, however there are long-term environmental impacts associated with them. Improper disposal of heavy metals can lead to safety hazards for people and the environment. The process of extracting sodium is much more environmentally friendly, making it a favorable option. |

# Detailed Explanation

Each material has its pros and cons and these should all be measured when assessing its practicality. As batteries seek to facilitate a greener and cleaner future, the processes which this is achieved should also be examined. Sustainability should be considered alongside cost and battery chemistries in order to find balanced and viable alternatives for batteries.
