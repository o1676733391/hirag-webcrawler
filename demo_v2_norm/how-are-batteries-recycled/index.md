# Overview

Battery recycling is crucial in addressing battery waste management. With the number of electronic devices being discarded each year, and the number of EV batteries reaching end-of-life increasing in the coming years, proper recycling will ensure that cells are taken care of properly, protecting people and the environment from the adverse effects of ill-disposed batteries. Even though a battery may no longer be usable, this does not mean that the materials within the cell are useless. On average, 80% of lead in new lead-acid batteries is recycled, showing that there is great potential for reusing materials.

# Detailed Explanation

Battery recycling is essential for managing battery waste effectively. The recycling process varies between different types of batteries, such as lead-acid and lithium-ion batteries.

## Lead-acid batteries

The recycling process for lead-acid batteries is well-established. These batteries are composed of lead, a plastic coating, and sulphuric acid. The recycling process involves breaking the batteries apart, neutralizing the sulphuric acid, and separating the lead and plastic. The lead and plastic can be reused in new batteries or other industrial products, while sodium sulphate is used in fertilizers and detergents.

## Lithium-ion batteries

Lithium-ion batteries can be recycled through two main processes:

1. **Pyrometallurgical recovery**: This method involves high temperatures to smelt the battery and recover metals like cobalt, nickel, and copper. However, this process burns away lithium, aluminum, and other organic compounds, resulting in a low recovery rate.

2. **Hydrometallurgical post-processing**: This method uses aqueous solutions to leach metals from the cathode. Lithium-ion cells are soaked in strong acids that dissolve the metals into a solution. This process is less energy-intensive and can recover lithium and copper, leading to a higher recovery rate. However, it requires caustic reagents such as hydrochloric, nitric, sulfuric acids, and hydrogen peroxide.

Some recyclers, like Redwood, combine both methods to maximize material recovery. They first heat the batteries to separate the metals and then use hydrometallurgical processes to recover individual compounds. This method reportedly allows for a 95-98% recovery rate for nickel, cobalt, copper, aluminum, and graphite, as well as over 80% of lithium.

Many scientists are exploring safer and more efficient ways to recover battery materials for recycling. For instance, one company is investigating the use of sulfur dioxide instead of hydrochloric acid or hydrogen peroxide for leaching cathode materials. The goal is to enable the extraction and reuse of all materials within a battery in the manufacturing process.
