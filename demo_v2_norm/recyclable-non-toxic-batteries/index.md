# Detailed Explanation

Most commercial batteries contain heavy metals, which can pose health and environmental risks if not disposed of properly. Metals inside batteries can be recovered and recycled, but the process is often complicated and unprofitable. As energy storage demand rises, there is a push for non-toxic and easily recyclable alternatives.

**What’s so toxic about batteries?**  
Batteries are generally safe while sealed, but exposure to the environment can release harmful metals. Lithium-ion batteries, while relatively safe, contain nickel and cobalt, which can accumulate and become toxic. They are also flammable, posing fire hazards. In Europe, lithium-ion batteries cannot be disposed of in landfills due to their toxicity. In the US, recycling plants have caught fire due to damaged batteries triggering thermal runaway events.

Lead-acid batteries, used since the 1910s, are recycled at a high rate, but lead is harmful when leaked. Cadmium, found in some batteries, is even more toxic. 

**Aren’t batteries being recycled now?**  
Currently, 90% of lead-acid batteries are recycled, but only 50% of lithium-ion batteries are. The cost of recycling lithium-ion batteries often exceeds the value of the retrieved materials. Companies are working to improve the recycling process, but it does not address the inherent toxicity of the materials used.

**Can batteries be non-toxic?**  
Researchers at Imperial College London have developed a battery prototype using plastic and salt water, which is non-toxic and recyclable. Although it has low energy density, it can charge and discharge quickly, making it suitable for applications like renewable energy grid storage. Other research is focused on creating nickel and cobalt-free batteries with safe liquid electrolytes.

**Conclusion**  
As concerns about mining and the environmental impact of battery materials grow, there is a significant push towards finding sustainable energy alternatives that support a safer solution for energy storage.
