# Overview

Nanotechnology is science at the nanoscale, working at the atomic or molecular level of matter. A nanometer is one billionth of a meter — invisible to the eye. Nanotechnology is being used to innovate in many different fields including medicine, the environment, technology, and energy storage. The concept of nanoscience first started in 1959 when physicist Richard Feynman spoke about manipulating and controlling individual atoms and molecules. The field as we now know it began in 1981, when the scanning tunneling microscope was developed, which allowed scientists to see individual atoms and begin research at the nanoscale. In the battery industry, many companies are working to incorporate nanotechnology into batteries and battery production processes in hopes of improving material mining and battery performance and capacity.

# Detailed Explanation

Nanotechnology is being utilized in various ways to enhance battery development. The advanced materials company TruSpin Nanomaterial Innovation employs silicon nanofibers to increase the energy capacity of lithium-ion batteries. Silicon is a favored choice for battery anodes due to its high ion-carrying capacity, but it tends to expand and shatter during charge/discharge cycles. By using nanofibers, TruSpin addresses these expansion issues, making the manufacturing process highly scalable and cost-efficient.

Amprius has also leveraged nanotechnology to make silicon more usable for batteries. They designed egg-like capsules to protect silicon from reacting negatively with the electrolyte, which can lead to the formation of a non-conductive solid-electrolyte interphase layer that reduces capacity. Their design allows for safe expansion and contraction of silicon, with a silicon nanoparticle surrounded by a highly conductive carbon shell that lithium ions can pass through. Their team found that the anode retained 74% of its capacity after 1000 charge/discharge cycles.

Pure lithium metal is another highly favored battery material, but it is highly reactive, leading to the formation of dendrites on the anode surface. To counteract this, scientists at Rice University have coated lithium metal foil with carbon nanotube film, which discourages lithium ions from latching onto the anode and prevents dendrite growth.

Despite its minuscule size, nanotechnology has the potential to significantly impact battery development, research, and manufacturing. High-quality battery test equipment allows researchers to identify patterns and gaps in battery performance, revealing areas where nanotechnology could provide solutions.
